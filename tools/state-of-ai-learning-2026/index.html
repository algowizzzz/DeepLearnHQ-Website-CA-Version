Not an industry survey — a practitioner's field report. After teaching generative AI to tens of thousands of complete beginners, the same patterns keep showing up. Here's what actually helps people get good with AI, and what quietly wastes their time.

Lesson 01

Most people want to use AI, not build it

The overwhelming majority of beginners aren't trying to train models — they want to write faster, work smarter, and build things. That path needs zero maths or coding. Confusing the two is the #1 reason people feel "behind."

Lesson 02

Prompting is the highest-leverage skill

The biggest jump in output quality comes from one habit: structuring requests as Role + Task + Context + Format. Same free model, dramatically better results. It beats memorizing any prompt list.

Lesson 03

Learning sticks when it's tied to a real goal

Abstract "learn AI" study fades fast. Learners who attach it to one real outcome — a report to automate, a landing page to write, a side project — retain far more and keep going.

Lesson 04

The "prompt engineer" title is cooling — the skill is rising

The standalone job title is fading as tools get better at messy prompts. But prompting inside your existing role — marketer, analyst, teacher, developer — is more valuable than ever. Don't chase the title; build the skill.

Lesson 05

Free tiers are enough to start

ChatGPT, Claude and Gemini all have genuinely capable free tiers. You don't need to pay to become useful with AI — pay only when you hit a real limit, not before.

Lesson 06

The best learners just started earlier

The people who are "good at AI" mostly aren't smarter or more technical — they started using it on real work sooner. Ten minutes of real use teaches more than forty explainer videos.
